Business Intelligence & Data Analytics Dashboard Builder

Exago BI



When you think of business intelligence (BI) and data analytics the first thing that likely comes to mind is a beautiful Dashboard. What isn’t thought of is the knowledge and work required to create this effortless-looking display of key metrics and charts. Exago’s Dashboard creation experience is outdated, confusing, and frustrating to use; overall a weak point for Exago in the competitive data analytics market.

Some competitors in the space are solely focused on Dashboards, and therefore often outshine Exago. Exago doesn't just focus on Dashboards, but it is still an important feature for customers and the sales team was losing deals because of it.

Business Goals



The sales team should focus on selling, not explaining.
Create an intuitive experience that does not confuse or distract potential customers from their most important question: Can Dashboards solve their problems?

Potential customers with a hard requirement for Dashboards would often buy competitor products.
Satisfy the expectation to quickly and easily build beautiful Dashboards like the competition.





User Needs



I watched test participants get lost within the interface over and over again. Multiple times per session.
Build a clear information architecture to shorten the learning curve, reduce time building Dashboards, and encourage exploration of features.





Where They Meet

The outdated interface affected both the sales team and the customer's credibility.

Consolidate and Simplify Filters


Four different filters mattered on a technical level, but from the user's perspective effectively did the same thing: limit data on the Dashboard.
I consolidated the filters and used consistent styling to convey a simple mental model. I also used progressive disclosure to hide options and information not immediately important. I instead highlighted and answered the three most pertinent questions at this point in the workflow:


What data is being filtered?

Where did the filter come from?

How do I create a filter?



Learnings


The user's mental model does not have to represent the system accurately for it to be usable. For example, a car steering wheel is made up of many parts, however, to steer a car the driver only needs to know how to turn the wheel.

Information Architecture


The old information architecture caused extreme frustration, a heavy cognitive load, and slowed users down dramatically. Test after test, I watched people get lost in the interface. Almost everyone gave up trying to learn the information architecture and instead would click every section until they stumbled upon whatever they were looking for.
The new layout was grouped more intuitively, labeled clearly, and consistent with Exago's other products.



Old Organization



Sections were duplicated and stacked next to each other; one set for global options and another for tile-specific options.





New Organization



The updated layout communicates visual hierarchy, where options that affect the entire Dashboard are above content-specific options.

Learnings


Asking the right questions at the right time is the hardest part of usability testing. I caught myself asking leading questions, missing opportunities to ask questions, and speaking when I should have been quiet.


Oftentimes I didn’t dig into questions enough. I would ask something like “Why did you decide to click on X?” with a response like “I was looking for a way to change Y.” I would stop there, instead of continuing down the line of questioning with “How are you trying to change that?”


Visual Design


Customers embed the software into their products, so the style needs to feel neutral and blend in. Secondly, the interface needs to be as simple and minimal as possible because the powerful solution can be easily overwhelming. Finally, I made a point to design accessible elements like labels, bigger text, and contrasting colors that pass WCAG standards.



Learnings


The marketing team's graphic designer collaborated with me in design sessions, greatly helping me improve my UI design skills. I learned to spend more time zoomed in to refine the small details as well as zoom out to the overall personality and make sure it is cohesive.

Outcomes


The long-term goals for the Dashboard Builder are to improve the overall user experience and contend better against competitors.

In one word, the experience was untrustworthy. Certain areas were difficult to navigate, confusing, and users would get unexpected results. On top of this, the application was sometimes buggy and slow, causing a lot of frustration (not something that ended up affecting my decisions but did affect the overall project scope).

Post-project, some indicators pointed out that we had improved these areas.

I did a round of usability testing with coworkers at the end of the project. Task completion rates improved and people found the options they were looking for at a much faster rate compared to usability testing conducted before the project.

We have received positive sentiment from customers and potential buyers regarding Dashboards; including more G2 Crowd reviews as well as one industry expert comparing Exago to a competitor tool claiming, "Exago Dashboards are much more intuitive and easy to use."

In addition to the project's successes, the team held a retrospective meeting to discuss learnings from the project. We collectively agreed to introduce more testing in the future - a huge win. I proved the value of usability testing and have since been given more room for research. I have also heard other team members suggest testing to answer some design questions.
